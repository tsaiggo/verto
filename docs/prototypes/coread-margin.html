On Reading Together

Essay · 6 min read, annotated with AI

For most of its history, reading was a conversation. Medieval manuscripts left wide margins not for decoration but for argument, and generations of readers wrote back to the text, and to each other, in the white space around the words.

The printed book quietly closed that space. Margins narrowed, the page became a finished surface, and the reader was recast from participant to audience. We kept the highlighter, but we lost the dialogue.

What changes now is that the other voice in the margin can answer. An attentive reader can ask a question of a sentence and receive not a definition but a reading, a second mind that has also read the whole page, holding the context you hold.

This is not annotation as storage. It is annotation as memory: a record of how two readers, one human and one machine, came to understand a text together, kept so the next text can begin where this one ended.

The shift sounds small, almost clerical. But a margin that can reply changes what reading is for. You no longer read to extract and file away; you read to think with something, and the thinking is what gets saved.

Try it on this very page. Select a sentence that resists you, and ask. The answer will appear where the question was asked, beside the words that prompted it, and it will still be here the next time you return.
